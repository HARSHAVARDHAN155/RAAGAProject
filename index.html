Flute

One of the most distinctive musical instruments is the flute. It's not only the oldest instrument; every civilization in the world uses it, making it the sole musical instrument.   The simple flute had several functions, including communication between and within tribes, with livestock, and most notably, as a kind of musical entertainment. The majority of flutes are primarily made of a tube with a mouthpiece on one end. The mouthpiece's oval-shaped hole is blown across by the performer while holding the flute horizontally. The performer simultaneously depresses the flute's keys, which are levers or holes
The Indian Flute was separately evolved from its western counterpart and is often constructed of bamboo. The seventh hole on the Indian Classical flute is additional compared to its folk counterpart to allow the full spectrum of traditional ragas. Further techniques were developed by Pandit Raghunath Prasanna to enable the flute to properly replicate the intricacies and complexities of Indian Classical music. In addition to the Indian Classical Flute, the bansuri is a common instrument in North Indian Hindustani music. It has six finger holes and one embouchure hole. Cross-fingering is a technique used by South Indian Carnatic musicians when playing the eight-holed velu

We 'pluck' selected notes from a scale that make up a chord - often the first, third, and fifth notes (and eighth note or octave). Broken chords Take it a step further and repeat the arpeggio notes beginning on each step of the arpeggio. In thirds scales, an interval known as a 'third' is introduced between each note of the scale.

Q. Do you prefer slow-based compositions or fast compositions? 
Start with slow and gradually increase the tempo and raise the climax and end it it high. Vilambit is an introductory slow tempo, or laya, between 25 and 75 beats per minute, used in the performance of a raga in Hindustani classical music. For major ragas, the vilambit portion generally takes up two-thirds or more of the performance.The composition starts in Vilambith, then enters madhyalay and then drut and finally ends in Ati drut. 

Vilambit is an introductory slow tempo, or lia, between 25 and 75 beats per minute. It is used in the performance of raga in Hindustani classical music. Vilambit portion generally takes up to two-thirds or more of the performance 

Madhya lia or Madhyalaya is a medium tempo, or lia, between 75 and 150 beats per minute in the Hindustandi classical music 

Drut is the section, in fast tempo, between 160 and 300 beats per minute in the Hindustandi classical music Ati drut is the concluding section which is of very fast tempo, between 300 and 600 beats per minute.

Since the beginning of time, India has mastered the art of classical singing. Indian classical music is mentioned multiple times in the earliest Hindu scriptures. In India, classical music is not seen as a means of amusement but rather as a way to communicate with holy beings and recognise their existence inside ourselves. Therefore, listening to classical music is more like using a tool for doing so. The distinctive tunes, commonly referred to as "ragas," are supposed to trigger the "chakras," which are the energy centres or emotions in our bodies.
The core pitch (Sa) that each vocalist choose allows their voice to span three octaves because there is no fixed pitch structure in South Indian music. Typically, they stick to this sales spiel their whole lives. The selected pitch is played during a performance as a drone on a tambura (a plucked string instrument), or in more recent times, on a sruti box (a bellows-blown reed instrument, now often electronic). Traditionally, all learning is done by ear and memory as repertoire and style are passed down from guru to pupil. Before reaching a professional level, instruction may entail regular classes and practise over an extended period of ten to twenty years. Even though, a vocalist often studies for their whole lifespan.

Violin is a wooden instrument played entirely with strings. The body of most violins is made of hollow wood. It is the smallest and most often used instrument in the family (soprano)  Typically, the violin has four strings (some can have five) Additionally, it may be played by pinching the strings with the fingers (pizzicato), and in some circumstances, the wooden side of the bow can be used to strike the strings.
Since the violin's invention in the early 16th century, there has been a long history of experimentation. First, the makers of the violin themselves empirically improved it over more than 200 years to reach its pinnacle of excellence in the late 17th and 18th centuries. Then, since the early 19th century, scientists have studied the violin's vibrational properties.
Aside from having a distinctive tone that distinguishes it from other instruments, the violin is one of the most significant instruments in Western classical music due to its exceptional musical agility, which allows it to play intricate note sequences in quick succession.
Picking the proper strings for a violin is crucial. Low-quality strings will produce cacophonous noises when a violinist attempts to stroke the strings,On a violin, each string produces a different tone. Because metal has the maximum tension and can produce the highest frequency sound, the E string, which is the highest note, is typically constructed of metal. While the G string, the lowest note, is composed of gut core string, which has far less tension than metal and produces low frequencies, the G string is constructed of metal.

TYPES OF VIOLIN


  • TYPE 1

    Modern violin 

    A contemporary violin is the most popular variety of violin. also known as acoustic violin The wooden sound box, which has a hollow body, is in charge of echoing the sound produced by the bow and the strings. Modern violins are played with a Tourte bow and contain metal and synthetic strings.


  • TYPE 2

    Baroque violin

    Baroque violins are played with gut strings and a bow in the baroque style. The proportions of the bass bar, bridge, fingerboard, neck, and tailpiece are different from those of modern violins. They are frequently played without a chin rest or shoulder rest. The baroque instrument's narrower neck angle reduces stress on the bridge and strings. There is also a shorter fingerboard.Despite these differences, a violin from the baroque and modern periods appear identical. Where they truly differ is in their sound. A baroque violin typically has a softer tone and is tuned lower.


  • TYPE 3


    Hardanger violin 

    Although it has a modern violin-like appearance, it is different from a 4 string violin in that it contains 8 or 9 strings. While the remaining strings reverberate from them, four of them are played similarly to a violin.A Hardanger Fiddle looks different from a violin because it is constructed of a thinner wood. They are frequently heavily embellished with ornaments made of black ink and carvings of animals.

CARNATIC INDICTION OF VIOLIN

In the late 18th and early 19th centuries, the violin was first used in Carnatic music. The violin gained popularity in the Carnatic language both as a solo instrument and as an accompaniment. Numerous master violinists have been refining the necessary methods since the 19th century. In contrast to western music, the violin is tuned differently in carnatic music. Similar to this, there are other ways to hold the violin. Contrary to Western music, Indian classical music does not prohibit the use of the left and right hands in transposition. There are variations in bow-holding techniques and other elements among different pedagogical institutions; nonetheless, it would be difficult to identify and select the optimal way. Based on the western manner of playing the violin  there has been a harmonization made in terms of holding the instrument and the participation of the right and left hand.

A carnatic song is composed using the notes "Sa" (Shadja) and "Pa." (Panchamam). The "Adhara Shadja Mam" is chosen by a vocalist or an instrumentalist based on their respective instruments.According to swara Sa, all swaras connected to one octave are in harmony (Shadja). The samvadi swara of "Sa" is panchama swara (Pa). The violin consists of four strings. The first string, which we refer to as "G," is tuned as "Mandra Sthai "Sa," followed by "D," "A," "Madhyamasthay "Sa," and "E," which is tuned as "Madhyamasthay "Pa." The four strings on top are tuned appropriately.
Violin students must tune their instruments to "Sa" "Pa" "Sa" "Pa" or "D" "A" "D" "A'' using a pitch pipe, electronic shruti box, electronic keyboard, piano picking the pitch, or adhara shadja man. For violin solo performances in Carnatic music, Adharashadjamam is typically played in the keys of D, E, or F.
